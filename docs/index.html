RFCScope: Detecting Logical Ambiguities in Internet Protocol Specifications

ASE 2025  ·  IEEE/ACM

RFCScope is the first scalable framework for detecting logical ambiguities in Internet Standards Track RFCs — the documents that define how the Internet works — using a modular LLM pipeline grounded in a systematic study of 11 years of verified technical errata.

Background

Why does this matter?

Internet protocol specifications, published as Requests for Comments (RFCs) by the IETF, are essential to ensuring the interoperability, security, and reliability of the Internet. However, ambiguities in these specifications — particularly logical ambiguities such as inconsistencies and under-specifications — can lead to critical misinterpretations and implementation errors. Unfortunately, such ambiguities remain largely overlooked and challenging to detect with existing tools.

🤖 Why LLMs are a promising fit

LLMs can read and reason over heterogeneous formats — natural language, formal notations, pseudocode, diagrams, tables — and apply broad domain knowledge. They can also combine information from different sources and can apply common networking knowledge. This makes them well-suited for detecting logical ambiguities in RFCs.

⚠️ Challenges of applying LLMs

  • RFCs are often lengthy and exceed LLM input limits
  • Ambiguity detection requires reasoning across multiple interrelated documents
  • LLMs lack specialized domain knowledge about logical ambiguities in Internet protocols
  • LLMs are prone to hallucinating plausible but incorrect conclusions

Empirical Study

A taxonomy of logical ambiguities

Through manual analysis of 273 verified technical errata from Standards Track RFCs (Jan 2014 – Jan 2025), we identified seven fine-grained subtypes of logical ambiguities in Standards Track RFCs.

Methodology

How RFCScope works

A four-stage LLM pipeline that constructs targeted context, semantically partitions specs, detects bugs with type-aware prompts, and filters hallucinations through structured re-validation.

End-to-end overview of the RFCScope pipeline, from raw RFC input to verified errata reports.
1

Context Constructor

Selectively extracts relevant content from all referenced documents — both RFC and non-RFC — using semantic search and LLM-powered summarization. Only what matters gets passed forward.

2

Partitioner

Semantically segments the RFC along its natural section hierarchy, keeping each chunk within the LLM's context window while preserving logical coherence and cross-section dependencies.

3

Analyzer

Uses bug-type-aware prompts and chain-of-thought reasoning to detect potential ambiguities across all seven subtypes, producing detailed candidate errata reports.

4

Evaluator

A second LLM pass independently re-validates each finding from scratch, rejecting hallucinated or out-of-scope reports before the final manual review step.

The Context Constructor resolves reference tags to specific sections or keyphrases, then retrieves RFC section content via semantic search and summarizes non-RFC documents via web-enabled LLM.
Evaluation

Results

Evaluated on the 20 most recent DNS-related Proposed Standard RFCs. RFCScope covers all seven ambiguity subtypes and significantly outperforms the only applicable baseline.

Errata counts at each pipeline stage across the 20 target RFCs. The Analyzer produced 281 initial reports; the Evaluator filtered these to 137; manual inspection yielded 31 final findings.
Distribution of the 31 detected ambiguities across all seven subtypes — 17 inconsistencies and 14 under-specifications.
Ablation study: removing any single component substantially reduces detection. The Partitioner has the greatest impact, followed by the Context Constructor, then domain-specific knowledge. The only applicable baseline (Mahbub et al.) detected just 1 ambiguity vs. RFCScope's 31.
